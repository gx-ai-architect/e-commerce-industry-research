# Institutional Research Standards: What Makes Reports Trusted vs. Dismissed

Generated: 2026-03-18
Source: Research analysis across sell-side, independent, consulting, and open-source research models

---

## 1. Sell-Side Equity Research (Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, JP Morgan)

### Standard Report Structure

1. **Cover page**: Ticker, rating (Buy/Sell/Hold), price target, analyst name, date, market data snapshot
2. **Investment thesis summary** (1 paragraph — the most-read element)
3. **Rating and price target rationale** with explicit valuation methodology (DCF, EV/EBITDA, sum-of-parts)
4. **Key catalysts and risks** — binary, time-bound events that could move the stock
5. **Financial model summary**: Revenue build (bottom-up by segment), margin bridge, EPS estimates vs. consensus
6. **Valuation section**: DCF with explicit WACC and terminal growth, comparable company table, historical trading range
7. **Industry/competitive analysis**
8. **Management quality assessment**
9. **Detailed financial statements** (3 years historical, 2-3 years projected)
10. **Disclosures and disclaimers**

### What Makes Them Authoritative

- **Analyst access**: Direct conversations with company management through earnings calls, NDRs, and private meetings. Sell-side analysts can call the CFO.
- **Proprietary financial models**: Multi-tab Excel models built and refined over years. Segment-level revenue builds, sensitivity tables, scenario analysis. The model IS the product.
- **Consensus-setting power**: Earnings estimates form "consensus" that moves stocks.
- **Track record**: Analysts ranked by Institutional Investor survey, TipRanks, StarMine. Credibility is personal and quantifiable.
- **Compliance infrastructure**: Legal review, fact-checking, regulatory disclosure.
- **Timeliness**: Reports published within hours of earnings, often before market open.

### Data Sources

- Company filings (10-K, 10-Q, 8-K, proxy)
- Earnings call transcripts (primary source, direct management quotes)
- Industry data providers (IDC, Gartner, comScore, Nielsen, SensorTower)
- Government data (Census Bureau, BLS, Federal Reserve)
- Proprietary surveys (Morgan Stanley AlphaWise, UBS Evidence Lab)
- Alternative data (credit card transactions, web traffic, satellite imagery, app downloads)
- Expert network calls (GLG, AlphaSights, Guidepoint)
- Channel checks (calling distributors, retailers, suppliers directly)
- Patent filings, job postings, regulatory filings

### Evidence Chain Pattern

**Claim → Data point → Source → Implication for estimates**

Example: "We believe AWS revenue will accelerate in Q3. Our proprietary survey of 200 CIOs shows 47% plan to increase cloud spend (vs. 38% last quarter), consistent with our channel checks indicating longer contract durations. We raise our Q3 AWS estimate to $23.1B (vs. consensus $22.4B)."

Every claim connects to a specific, numbered estimate change. Opinions are expressed as numbers.

### Information Density Techniques

- **Exhibit-heavy**: A 30-page report may contain 20+ charts/tables
- **Summary tables at top**: Key financial metrics in a standardized grid
- **"Variant perception" highlighted**: What the analyst believes differently from consensus
- **Scenario analysis**: Bull/base/bear with explicit probability weights
- **Margin bridges**: Waterfall charts showing expansion/compression drivers
- **Revenue build tables**: Bottom-up, segment-by-segment, geography-by-geography

## 2. Independent Research (SemiAnalysis, Stratechery, ARK Invest)

### What Gives Them Credibility Without Institutional Backing

1. **Domain expertise signal**: SemiAnalysis demonstrates knowledge of chip fabrication that most sell-side analysts cannot match. The technical depth IS the credential.
2. **Prediction track record**: Public and verifiable. Stratechery's framework predictions have been validated repeatedly over a decade.
3. **Original frameworks**: Creating new mental models signals deep thinking. Stratechery's Aggregation Theory is cited by executives, VCs, and academics.
4. **Source network transparency**: Readers infer source quality from the specificity of claims.
5. **Consistency over years**: Weekly publishing over 5-10+ years builds compounding credibility.
6. **Willingness to be wrong publicly**: Publishing corrections builds more trust than never admitting error.

### How They Differ from Sell-Side

- No company access advantage; compensate with deeper domain expertise
- No quarterly estimate game; focus on multi-year secular trends
- Longer time horizons (3-7 years vs. sell-side 12-month targets)
- More opinionated; no compliance department preventing bold claims
- Can be genuinely negative without fear of losing underwriting business
- Subscription-based revenue removes conflict of interest

### Evidence Chain Pattern

**Technical fact → Industry context → Strategic implication → Prediction**

Example (SemiAnalysis style): "TSMC's CoWoS packaging capacity is constrained to ~15K wafer starts/month. Given Nvidia's H100 requires ~2x the CoWoS area of A100, and demand implies 40K+ equivalent wafer starts, the bottleneck will persist through 2025. This means [specific companies] will face allocation challenges, and [specific alternatives] will gain share."

## 3. Consulting Research (McKinsey Global Institute, Bain, BCG)

### The "Structured Assertion" Format

The single most important technique in consulting research. Every section starts with an assertion in the header, supported by data in the body.

- BAD header: "Revenue by segment"
- GOOD header: "Digital segment now accounts for 60% of revenue growth, up from 12% five years ago"

### The Pyramid Principle (Barbara Minto)

Start with the answer, then provide supporting evidence in hierarchical layers. Never bury the lead.

### Analytical Frameworks

- 2x2 matrices (the iconic consulting visual)
- S-curves for technology adoption
- Value chain analysis
- TAM sizing with bottom-up methodology
- Industry profit pool analysis (Bain's signature)
- Scenario planning with named archetypes

### Data Visualization Rules

- Every chart has a "so what" annotation
- Highlight the key data point (bold, color, callout)
- Remove chartjunk ruthlessly
- Source every single data point
- Use the same color consistently for the same entity

## 4. What Makes Research Trusted vs. Dismissed

### Trust Signals

1. **Specificity over generality**: "Revenue grew 23% YoY to $4.2B" vs. "strong growth"
2. **Named sources and verifiable data**: "According to IDC's Q3 2025 report" vs. "industry reports suggest"
3. **Acknowledgment of uncertainty**: "The bear case shows 30% downside" vs. one-sided analysis
4. **Variant perception**: "We disagree with consensus because..." vs. restating consensus
5. **Original analytical work**: Proprietary surveys, original data analysis, model outputs
6. **Internal consistency**: Revenue → margins → cash flow → valuation all flow logically
7. **Author credibility**: Named analyst with track record

### What Makes Investors Dismiss a Report

1. **"Book report" syndrome**: Summarizing publicly available information without analytical value
2. **Circular reasoning**: Using price to justify fundamentals
3. **TAM abuse**: "$500B TAM" without bottom-up build
4. **False precision**: Projecting revenue to the dollar in year 5
5. **Missing the "so what"**: Data without interpretation
6. **Recency bias**: Extrapolating recent trends without considering base rates
7. **No variant perception**: If the report doesn't tell you something new, it has zero investment value
8. **Buzzword density**: "Synergy," "paradigm shift," "transformative" without substance
9. **Symmetrical bull/bear cases**: Equidistant from base signals no probability assessment
10. **No engagement with counter-arguments**: Ignoring the best argument against your thesis

## 5. Information vs. Insight

**INFORMATION**: A fact in the public domain.
> "Amazon's AWS revenue was $25.0B in Q3 2025."

**ANALYSIS**: Applying a framework to reveal a pattern.
> "AWS revenue growth reaccelerated to 29% YoY, the third consecutive quarter of acceleration."

**INSIGHT**: A non-obvious conclusion that changes how you think and has implications for action.
> "AWS's reacceleration is not driven by new workload migration (which has slowed) but by existing customers scaling AI inference workloads. This means AWS growth is now levered to AI adoption rates rather than cloud migration rates, fundamentally changing the growth trajectory. Consensus models still embed the old framework and are structurally too low."

### Techniques for Generating Insight

1. **Inversion**: Instead of "why will this work?" ask "what would have to be true for this to fail?"
2. **Base rate analysis**: What is the historical base rate for this outcome?
3. **Supply chain tracing**: Follow the money upstream and downstream
4. **Variant perception identification**: State consensus and where you disagree
5. **Mechanism identification**: Explain causation, not just correlation
6. **Time horizon arbitrage**: Most focus on 12 months. Insight often comes from 3-5 year view worked backward

## 6. Anti-Patterns in AI-Generated Research

1. **"Confident summary" problem**: Fluent text that restates public information with no edge
2. **Hallucinated specificity**: Plausible but fabricated statistics
3. **"Both sides" hedging**: Real research takes positions
4. **Structural uniformity**: Suspiciously uniform section lengths and parallel structures
5. **Absence of primary sources**: AI cannot conduct interviews or run surveys
6. **Temporal confusion**: Mixing data from different periods without noting it
7. **Missing analytical spine**: Collection of loosely related sections without unifying thesis
8. **Generic recommendations**: "Companies should invest in AI" is not a recommendation
9. **Over-reliance on lists**: Core arguments need analytical prose, not bullet points
10. **No "ugly" data**: Real research includes data that complicates the thesis

## 7. Design Principles for Automated Research Agent

### Sourcing Principles
- Cite specific sources for every quantitative claim
- Distinguish primary data (gathered/computed) from secondary data (citing others)
- Include data recency — always note when data was collected/published
- When data is unavailable, say so explicitly rather than fabricating
- Cross-reference: if two independent sources agree, note it; if they disagree, explain why

### Analytical Principles
- Build an analytical spine: every section advances the core argument
- Include counter-arguments — the strongest version, not a strawman
- Show your work: assumptions behind every estimate
- Quantify uncertainty: ranges, scenarios, sensitivity analysis over point estimates

### Anti-Hallucination Principles
- Never generate specific statistics without a verified source
- Flag low-confidence claims: "We were unable to verify" is more trustworthy than unsourced confidence
- Note where sources disagree
- Prefer conservative estimates — directionally right beats precisely wrong
