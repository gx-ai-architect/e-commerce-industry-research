## Executive Thesis

PDD Holdings released FY2025 earnings this morning: revenue RMB 431.8B (+10%), operating profit RMB 94.6B (-13%), net income RMB 99.4B (-12%). The stock rose 8.3% to $106.23. The market liked the Q4 acceleration — Q4 revenue +12%, Q4 operating profit +8.3% YoY — after three quarters of margin compression [E1, E2]. But the headline numbers obscure five questions that will determine whether PDD is worth $150B or $300B by year-end 2026.

This report is not a comprehensive company overview — our v5 report covers business mechanics, moat analysis, and management quality. This is a forward-looking note structured around five specific, answerable questions, each tied to evidence from today's earnings and the regulatory landscape that has shifted dramatically since February's Supreme Court tariff ruling [E3].

## Q1: Is Margin Compression Bottoming?

**Verdict: Yes, tentatively. Q4 is the inflection point.**

The quarterly trajectory tells the story that the annual numbers obscure [E1, E2]:

| Quarter | Operating Profit (RMB B) | YoY Change | Margin |
|---------|-------------------------|------------|--------|
| Q1 2025 | 16.1 | -38% | 16.8% |
| Q2 2025 | 25.8 | -21% | 24.8% |
| Q3 2025 | 25.0 | +2% | 23.1% |
| Q4 2025 | 27.7 | **+8.3%** | 22.4% |
| FY2025 | 94.6 | -13% | 21.9% |

Q1 was the trough. Each successive quarter improved. By Q4, operating profit is growing again — even as management continues investing aggressively [E4].

### What's Driving the Cost Increase

FY2025 cost of revenue surged 23% to RMB 188.8B, compressing gross margin 460 basis points (60.9% → 56.3%) [E2, E5]. The press release attributes this to three factors without quantifying each:

1. **Fulfillment fees** — Temu's warehouse buildout and logistics costs. This is structural: as Temu shifts from cross-border (cheap air freight, no warehousing) to semi-managed (local warehouses, carrier contracts), fulfillment costs rise permanently. 13 warehouses globally, with a 14th planned in Brazil [E6].

2. **Bandwidth and server costs** — Platform infrastructure scaling. This should moderate as a percentage of revenue.

3. **Payment processing fees** — Scales with transaction volume. Roughly proportional.

R&D surged 30% to RMB 16.5B (3.8% of revenue, up from 3.2%) [E2]. Management emphasized "supply chain modernization" without specifics. S&M grew only 13% — disciplined relative to revenue growth. G&A actually declined 12% [E5].

### The Interest Income Floor

Here's what most analysts miss: PDD earned RMB 25.6B ($3.7B) in interest and investment income in FY2025, up 24% from RMB 20.6B in FY2024 [E7]. This is 27% of operating profit and explains why net income (RMB 99.4B) exceeded operating profit (RMB 94.6B) despite FX losses. The total liquid asset base — cash, short-term investments, restricted cash, and non-current deposits — reached RMB 600.8B [E7, E8].

This creates an earnings floor. Even if operating profit declines another 10%, interest income growth (+24% YoY) partially offsets. Management could literally stop growing the operating business and PDD would still earn ~$14B/year.

### 2026 Margin Outlook

Management explicitly warned: "These investments are firm and long-term, and will inevitably affect our financial performance" [E4]. But the Q4 trajectory suggests the RMB 100B merchant program's heaviest impact was in H1 2025 (Q1 operating profit -38%). If the program spending is roughly linear over 3 years (~RMB 33B/year), the margin drag stabilizes rather than worsens.

**Our estimate:** FY2026 operating profit flat to +5%, with interest income growth providing net income growth of 5-10%. Analyst consensus expects a 25% earnings rebound — we think that's aggressive unless Temu margins improve faster than expected [E9].

## Q2: Does the SCOTUS Ruling Revive Temu's US Business?

**Verdict: Partially. The tariff burden halved, but the structural shift to semi-managed is irreversible.**

### What SCOTUS Actually Changed

On February 20, 2026, the Supreme Court struck down all IEEPA-based tariffs in a 6-3 decision (*Learning Resources v. Trump*) [E3, E10]. This eliminated:
- The 10% "fentanyl tariff" on all Chinese imports
- The 125% reciprocal tariffs on China
- Tariffs on Canada, Mexico, and other countries under IEEPA

What REMAINS in effect:
- **Section 301:** 7.5-25% on most Chinese goods (product-dependent)
- **Section 232:** Steel, aluminum, copper, autos, semiconductors
- **Section 122:** 15% global surcharge (effective Feb 24, expires **July 24, 2026**)
- **De minimis:** Permanently dead. Not restored by SCOTUS. Statutory repeal via OBBB effective July 2027 [E10, E11]

### Net Tariff Impact

The Yale Budget Lab estimates SCOTUS cut the effective tariff rate on Chinese goods by roughly two-thirds: from ~16% to ~9.1% average [E10]. For a typical Temu product, the effective rate dropped from ~120-145% to ~30-45% (Section 301 + Section 122 + MFN duty) [E12].

This is material but not transformative. A $10 product that cost $24.50 with IEEPA tariffs now costs ~$14.50. Better, but not the $10 it was pre-tariff when de minimis was alive [E12].

### Has Temu's US Business Actually Recovered?

The evidence is mixed:
- **Volume:** Temu US daily packages dropped ~70%, from ~1M/day to ~250-300K/day. No evidence of recovery post-SCOTUS yet (ruling was 4 weeks ago) [E6, E13]
- **Users:** US MAU collapsed 76% from ~34M to ~8M (web visitors). App-based MAU at 133.6M as of October 2025 — these are different metrics [E14]
- **Ad spend:** Temu resumed US advertising — 1,100 Meta ads, 700 Google ads active. Re-entered App Store top ranks [E14]
- **Model shift:** Semi-managed orders now represent 80% of US sales. 70% of platform traffic directed to semi-managed SKUs [E6, E15]
- **Pricing:** Prices partially recovered via US warehouse fulfillment and 18% cuts on bestsellers, but NOT to pre-tariff levels [E16]

**The structural shift is irreversible.** Even if SCOTUS had restored de minimis, Temu has already invested in 13 warehouses and rebuilt its supply chain around local fulfillment. The old "China-to-door via air freight" model is dead. Semi-managed IS the model now [E6].

### Where Temu IS Growing

The geographic pivot is working [E17, E18]:
- **EU MAU:** 141.6M (+74% YoY) — now larger than US
- **LatAm:** +122% YoY
- **EU GMV:** On track for $15-20B in 2025, potentially $25B+ in 2026
- **Global MAU:** 530M+ despite US decline

The critical upcoming date: **EU €3/item customs duty on July 1, 2026** [E19]. This is the European version of de minimis elimination. On a €5 item, €3 is a 60% cost increase. PDD is pre-positioning with 10 European warehouses and 80% local fulfillment target [E6].

## Q3: Has Pinduoduo's Domestic Ad Engine Peaked?

**Verdict: Yes, the growth engine is shifting from OMS to TS + instant retail.**

The deceleration is unmistakable [E2, E5]:

| Quarter | OMS Growth | TS Growth | Gap |
|---------|-----------|----------|-----|
| Q1 2025 | +15% | +6% | OMS leads |
| Q2 2025 | +13% | +1% | OMS leads (TS hit by tariffs) |
| Q3 2025 | +8% | +10% | TS overtakes |
| Q4 2025 | **+5%** | **+19%** | **14pp gap — TS accelerating** |

OMS (online marketing services — essentially advertising revenue from merchants) has decelerated from +15% to +5% over four quarters. This is not tariff-related; it's the domestic Pinduoduo business approaching ad monetization maturity. The domestic take rate at ~4.1% is nearing Alibaba's ~4.5-5% [E20]. There's headroom, but not much.

Meanwhile, transaction services (Temu commissions + payment processing) accelerated to +19% in Q4, driven by Temu's semi-managed model gaining traction and Q4 seasonal volume. The revenue mix hit parity for the first time: OMS 50.4% / TS 49.6% [E2].

### What Replaces OMS as the Growth Engine?

Three candidates:

**1. Take rate expansion** — Domestic take rate at ~4.1% has room to reach Alibaba's ~5%. That's ~20% upside on ~RMB 218B OMS revenue = ~RMB 44B additional revenue. But the SAMR pricing rules effective April 10, 2026 ban platforms from forcing lowest-price agreements and using algorithm penalties to coerce predatory pricing [E21]. This directly constrains PDD's ability to extract higher ad revenue through more aggressive merchant competition.

**2. Instant retail / Duoduo Maicai** — RMB 10B investment, 200 warehouses in Shanghai, Maicai GMV +340% YoY after Meituan Youxuan exited [E22, E23]. Goldman projects the instant commerce TAM at RMB 1.5T by 2030 [E23]. PDD is the last major community group buying platform standing. But Meituan has 6,000+ warehouses across 200 cities — PDD's 200 Shanghai warehouses are a starting point, not a competitive position.

**3. Temu transaction services** — If Temu's semi-managed model scales globally, TS revenue could grow 15-25% annually while OMS stagnates. This shifts the revenue quality profile: TS is lower-margin but higher-growth.

### The SAMR Algorithm Threat

The 29-article pricing regulation effective April 10, 2026 is the most significant domestic regulatory change for PDD [E21, E24]. It bans:
- Forcing merchants to accept "lowest price on the internet" terms
- Using traffic throttling or search demotion to coerce predatory pricing
- Algorithmic price discrimination without consumer consent

This directly targets PDD's core mechanism: the feed-first algorithm that rewards the cheapest merchant with free traffic. PDD had already dropped the explicit "全网最低价" requirement during 618 2025, replacing it with "同款同价" (same product, same price) [E24]. But the regulation formalizes the restriction. The question is enforcement: if SAMR actively polices algorithm-driven price-forcing, PDD's structural pricing advantage erodes.

**Our view:** The regulation constrains PDD's most aggressive pricing tactics but doesn't kill the algorithm. PDD's advantage shifts from "forced lowest price" to "best demand aggregation" — a subtler but still defensible moat. The regulation actually benefits PDD's "High-Quality Development" pivot by providing regulatory cover for reducing price-war intensity.

PDD has the largest total liquid asset position of any e-commerce company globally and returns zero to shareholders [E8, E25, E26]. Management's Q4 call offered no change: "supply chain investment is where we will place our greatest conviction... with an all-in mindset" [E4].

The most telling data point: **cash grew RMB 90.7B ($13B) in FY2025** — even during the year management described as "heavy investment." The RMB 100B merchant program is spread over multiple years, not a single-year burn. PDD is still a massive cash generator despite the investment rhetoric.

### Where Is the Money Going Instead?

Five identified investment areas [E25, E27, E28]:

1. **Temu warehouse network** — 13 warehouses, 14th planned. This is the primary CoR driver (+23%).
2. **RMB 100B merchant/farmer program** — Fee reductions, logistics subsidies, "New Quality Supply." Three-year commitment.
3. **R&D** — RMB 16.5B (+30%). Supply chain automation, algorithm improvements.
4. **Instant retail** — RMB 10B for 200 warehouses, Maicai integration. "Free delivery to villages."
5. **Duoduo Maicai recovery** — Absorbing Meituan Youxuan's exit, GMV +340%.

### The Li Lu Signal

The most significant capital allocation data point isn't from management — it's from Li Lu. Himalaya Capital (Li Lu's fund, mentored by Charlie Munger) built a $482M position in PDD in Q2 2025 at ~$105/share and held through Q4. PDD is now 14.6% of Himalaya's $3.57B portfolio — the largest position. Li Lu concentrates: 75% of his portfolio is in just 3 stocks [E29].

Meanwhile, Tiger Global exited 100%, Mirae Asset sold 99.1% (-$4.1B), Goldman Sachs sold 41.9% (-$764M), and Fidelity sold 45.9% (-$1.33B) [E29]. The positioning split is stark: value/long-term investors entering, momentum/growth investors fleeing.

**Our view:** A buyback or dividend is unlikely in 2026 unless the stock drops significantly below $90 (where Li Lu bought). Management views reinvestment ROI as superior to capital return. This is either Bezos-style reinvestment (Amazon returned nothing for 20 years) or value-destructive hoarding. Li Lu's conviction — 14.6% of a Munger-school portfolio — suggests the former.
