# Chinese E-Commerce: The Traffic Tax, the Subsidy Trap, and the Regulatory Reset

*Published March 23, 2026 | Industry Deep Research Report v2*

---

## Executive Thesis

Chinese e-commerce is not in decline. It is in the middle of three overlapping wars that have temporarily destroyed industry profitability — and all three wars have identifiable expiration dates.

The first war is over merchant economics. Taobao/Tmall merchants pay 20-40% of revenue in all-in costs, with traffic/advertising alone consuming over 50% of operating costs [E4][E12]. Pinduoduo merchants can operate at 5-7% [E5]. This 4-6x cost gap explains why merchants are migrating, why Douyin's shelf commerce grew 49% YoY [E46], and why Alibaba's Customer Management Revenue growth decelerated from 12% to 1% in four quarters [E1]. The second war is food delivery: combined S&M spending hit RMB 102.7B in Q2 2025 alone, with Meituan swinging from RMB 35.8B profit to a projected RMB 24.3B loss [E18][E19]. The third is over consumer loyalty — the "refund-only" policy race to the bottom that produced a 50% after-sales rate for some merchants before regulators forced all platforms to reverse course by July 2025 [E41].

We believe these wars end in 2026. SAMR's Platform Pricing Rules take effect April 2026. The State Council's Anti-Monopoly Committee targeted food delivery "internal competition" in January 2026 [E23]. The refund-only reversal is already complete [E42]. Beijing is imposing a ceasefire. The platforms that survive with the best merchant unit economics and the strongest organic demand — not the ones that burned the most cash — will dominate the next cycle.

## Q1: What Does It Actually Cost to Sell on a Chinese E-Commerce Platform?

The public debate about Chinese e-commerce platform costs obsesses over commission rates. This misses the real story entirely. Commissions are 0.6-5% depending on the platform. The actual pain point — traffic/advertising costs — runs 10-30% of revenue on Taobao/Tmall and accounts for over half of a typical merchant's total operating budget [E4][E12].

### The All-In Cost Breakdown

| Cost Component | Taobao/Tmall | Pinduoduo (price-competitive) | JD.com (3P) | Douyin |
|---------------|-------------|-------------------------------|-------------|--------|
| Commission/Service Fee | 0.6% base + 2-5% Tmall | 0.6-1% tech fee | 2-8% (some 0.6%) | ~2-5% |
| Traffic/Advertising | 10-30% of revenue | Algorithmic (low if cheapest) | Varies | Content-driven, high |
| After-sales/Logistics | ~5% | Included in model | Varies | Subsidized |
| **All-In Range** | **20-40%+** | **5-7% (best) to 12-27%** | **~2.4% take rate** | **~10% all-in** |

*Sources: [E4][E5][E6][E8][E13][E14]*

The commission-to-traffic income ratio on Taobao/Tmall is approximately 1:2.4 [E4]. For every yuan Alibaba collects in commissions, it collects 2.4 yuan in advertising fees. This is not a marketplace; it is an advertising platform that happens to process transactions.

One lamp factory owner's case illustrates the squeeze: daily promotion spending rose from 100 RMB to 400-500 RMB, with 30-day promotion fees reaching 11,400 RMB — consuming nearly 70% of her profits [E7]. A fashion brand founder reported return rates rising from 30-40% to 70-80% since 2021 while traffic costs increased tenfold [E4]. These are not edge cases. Multiple merchants report traffic promotion fees at 50%+ of their cost structure, some reaching 70% [E7].

### Why PDD's Cost Structure Is Structurally Different

Pinduoduo's algorithmic traffic distribution gives merchants who offer the lowest price free visibility. This flips Taobao's model: instead of paying for traffic, merchants invest in price reductions and receive organic reach. SME merchants on PDD can redirect approximately 15% of marketing expenses into lower prices, earning algorithmic recommendations [E5]. The result: best-case all-in costs of 5-7% versus 20-40% on Taobao. Even non-price-competitive PDD merchants face 12-27% — still meaningfully below Taobao's floor [E5].

PDD charges a technical service fee of 0.6-1% of GMV, introduced in 2020 [E11]. Chairman Chen Lei pledged a 10 billion yuan fee waiver for "high-quality" merchants, explicitly accepting short-term profitability declines [E11]. PDD's Q1 2025 operating profit fell 38% YoY to RMB 16.1B as these investments hit the P&L [E17].

### Alibaba's Monetization Pivot

Alibaba made a structural shift on September 1, 2024: a 0.6% software service fee on all Taobao and Tmall transactions, replacing the fixed annual fee (30,000-60,000 yuan) previously charged to Tmall merchants [E9]. Taobao C2C stores — previously exempt from transaction-based fees — are now paying for the first time. Small merchants earning less than 120,000 yuan annually receive full refunds [E9].

This fee boosted CMR growth to 12% in Q4 FY2025 (March 2025) and 10% in Q1-Q2 FY2026 [E1]. But the base effect faded: CMR grew only 1% YoY in Q3 FY2026 (December 2025) [E1]. The more important monetization lever is Quanzhantui ("full-site promotion"), now at ~30% penetration and expected to reach 45-50% in FY2026 [E10]. This tool merges search, recommendation, and display advertising into a unified bidding system — effectively raising the floor for merchant advertising spend.

### JD's Take Rate Anomaly

JD.com's overall take rate of approximately 2.4% on GMV appears low but is misleading [E6]. The hybrid 1P/3P model (1P 27%, 3P 73% of GMV in 2024) means 1P revenue inflates the revenue line while producing thin margins. 3P commission rates range from 2-8% depending on category, with some POP merchants in the low-price strategy program reduced to 0.6% [E6]. JD Worldwide sellers face a $15,000 refundable deposit and $1,000 annual platform fee [E6].

### Douyin's All-In Cost: The Content Tax Replaces the Traffic Tax

Douyin's approximately 10% all-in merchant cost [E8] appears moderate, but the cost structure is qualitatively different from Taobao's. On Taobao, merchants buy visibility through search ads and display placements — a direct cash outlay. On Douyin, merchants "pay" through content production: short videos, livestreams, influencer partnerships. The cash cost is lower, but the operational complexity is higher. A merchant who can produce compelling content gets organic distribution for free. A merchant who cannot must buy traffic through Douyin's advertising system at rates comparable to Taobao's.

Douyin invested 32 billion yuan in merchant cost savings in 2025 [E14][E49], including commission waivers for product cards, promotion fee rebates, and reduced shipping insurance. This subsidy intensity — 32B yuan on an estimated 3.5-4T yuan GMV base — represents roughly 0.8-0.9% of GMV in direct merchant support. Compare this to PDD's 10B yuan fee waiver pledge on a larger GMV base [E11]. Douyin is spending more per GMV yuan to attract and retain merchants than any other platform.

### The Regulatory Response

SAMR released a 28-article "Compliance Guide for Online Trading Platform Fees" in May 2025 [E15]. Key provisions:

- Platforms must publish commission fees prominently
- 7-day public opinion solicitation period before pricing changes
- 3-year retention of historical pricing records
- Ban on duplicate charging, passing costs to merchants, and unreasonable security deposits

The comment period ended June 3, 2025, and the rules take effect April 2026 [E15]. This is the first time Beijing has directly regulated the fee structure of e-commerce platforms. Combined with the Anti-Unfair Competition Law revision (passed June 2025), which bans platforms from forcing merchants to sell below cost, the regulatory framework constrains both the platforms' ability to extract fees and their ability to subsidize prices.

### What This Means for Platform Economics

The merchant cost data reveals a fundamental tension in Chinese e-commerce: the platforms that charge the most (Taobao at 20-40%) are not necessarily the ones earning the highest margins. Alibaba's CMR of RMB 102.7B in Q3 FY2026 [E1] comes from a monetization machine that extracts 4-5% of GMV through commissions plus advertising [E8]. PDD's total revenue of RMB 108.3B in Q3 2025 [E16] comes from a mix of transaction services and online marketing on a different (undisclosed) GMV base. The cost difference to merchants is real, but the revenue to platforms is converging. The question is which model produces a more sustainable merchant ecosystem — and the Heimao complaint data (Q4) provides a damning answer.

## Q2: How Much Has the Food Delivery War Burned, and Can Anyone Win?

The food delivery subsidy war that erupted in early 2025 is the most expensive competitive battle in Chinese internet history. In Q2 2025 alone, Alibaba, JD, and Meituan's combined sales and marketing expenses totaled RMB 102.7B — Alibaba 53.2B, JD 27B, Meituan 22.5B [E18]. Combined profits fell by nearly RMB 80B ($11.27B) versus the prior year across Q2-Q3 2025 [E18].

### The Damage by Company

**Meituan** absorbed the worst hit. Full-year 2025 net loss is projected at 23.3-24.3 billion yuan ($3.5B), versus a 35.8 billion yuan profit in 2024 [E19]. Core Local Commerce swung from 52.4B yuan profit to 6.8B yuan loss [E19]. Q3 2025 operating loss was 19.8B yuan ($2.7B), the largest since listing. Sales and marketing expenses surged 90.9% YoY to 34.3B yuan in Q3 — reaching 35.9% of revenue versus 19.2% in the prior year [E19]. This is a company that went from 85% market share and comfortable profitability to defending a 55-58% share at enormous cost [E22].

**Alibaba** announced a RMB 50B ($7B) subsidy plan for Taobao Flash Buy in July 2025 [E21]. The impact: Alibaba's China e-commerce adjusted EBITA decreased 76% in Q2 FY2026 (September quarter), "primarily due to investment in quick commerce, user experiences, and technology" [E2]. Alibaba's free cash flow swung to a net outflow of 18.815B yuan, from an inflow of 17.372B yuan in the same period of 2024 [E21]. On August 7, during the "First Autumn Milk Tea" promotion, Taobao's DAU outpaced Pinduoduo's by over 100 million in a single day [E22] — proof that food delivery drives DAU for the broader e-commerce platform.

**JD.com** entered food delivery last, with founder Richard Liu staging a publicity stunt delivering food in Beijing [E20]. Nomura estimated JD's food delivery push generated a loss of more than 10 billion yuan in Q2 2025 alone [E20]. JD announced a 10 billion yuan first round of subsidies [E20]. JD.com's Q3 2025 net service revenues grew 30.8% YoY [E27], reflecting rapid marketplace scaling, but at ruinous cost.

### Per-Order Economics

The unit economics reveal why this war is unsustainable:

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| National average order value (AOV) | RMB 50-65 (~$7-9) |
| Rider cost per order | RMB 7-9 (14-18% of AOV) |
| Total 2024 food delivery GMV | RMB 1.6 trillion |
| Total rider-related costs | RMB 80-100 billion |
| Meituan daily orders | ~70 million |
| Meituan per-order loss (from Jun 2025) | 1.4-3 yuan/order |

*Sources: [E24][E25]*

Meituan's food delivery unit economics turned negative around May 2025, with estimated losses of 1.4 yuan per order starting in June and expanding to approximately 2-3 yuan per order by Q3 [E25]. Adding to the pressure: local governments began implementing mandatory social insurance for riders in 2025, adding RMB 0.3-0.5 per order in labor costs [E25][E26].

The structural outlook is grim. By 2030, rider costs could rise to 40-60% of order value if current trends continue, making the current delivery model unsustainable. A 30-yuan meal today would need to be priced at 45-50+ yuan to preserve platform unit economics [E26].

### Market Share Restructuring

The war produced a dramatic reshuffling:

| Player | Pre-War Share | Current Share |
|--------|-------------|---------------|
| Meituan | ~85% | 55-58% |
| Taobao Flash Buy (Ele.me rebrand) | ~15% | 35-37% |
| JD.com | 0% | 5-8% |

*Source: [E22]*

Meituan lost approximately 30 percentage points of share in under a year. Taobao Flash Buy more than doubled. JD carved out a small position. The question: is a 35-37% share worth RMB 50B in subsidies for Alibaba? We believe the answer is yes — but only if the subsidy war ends before Alibaba's cash position (RMB 460B+) is materially impaired, and only if the DAU gains from food delivery translate into sustained e-commerce engagement.

### Regulatory Ceiling

Regulators are losing patience. SAMR summoned JD, Meituan, and Ele.me in May 2025 over "irrational competition" [E23]. On January 9, 2026, the State Council's Anti-Monopoly and Anti-Unfair Competition Committee stated its intent to "combat internal competition" in the food delivery market [E23]. Coffee prices dropped to as low as 2 yuan ($0.28) on Meituan [E23] — a price that is plainly below cost and will not survive regulatory scrutiny.

Despite this, Alibaba stated on January 8, 2026 that its Taobao Flash Purchase strategy for 2026 is "continued increased investment with goal of achieving absolute market leadership" [E23]. Alibaba is betting that regulatory rhetoric will not translate into hard enforcement.

### The Structural Question: Is Food Delivery a Viable Business or a Permanent Subsidy?

The long-term outlook for food delivery unit economics is bleak. Rider costs currently consume 14-18% of order value at RMB 7-9 per order [E24]. Local governments are gradually implementing mandatory social insurance for riders starting 2025, adding RMB 0.3-0.5 per order in labor costs [E25][E26]. By 2030, one analysis projects rider costs could rise to 40-60% of order value [E26]. A 30-yuan meal today would need to be priced at 45-50+ yuan just to preserve platform unit economics [E26].

This math suggests the current food delivery model — low AOV, subsidized pricing, 30-minute delivery — may only last a decade in its current form. The platforms that are spending RMB 100B+ to win this market may be winning a business that will never generate adequate returns on invested capital. The counterargument: food delivery is not a standalone business but a customer acquisition channel. If Alibaba's August 7 milk tea promotion data is representative — DAU exceeding Pinduoduo's by 100M+ in a single day [E22] — then the cross-selling value may justify permanent subsidy. This is an unresolved question that will define Chinese tech profitability for years.

JD.com's FY2024 revenue of RMB 1,158.8B [E28] and Q3 2025 net service revenue growth of 30.8% [E27] show the broader business remains strong. The question is whether management discipline holds on food delivery. JD's FCF trajectory will be the key tell.

## Q3: How Is Temu Restructuring After De Minimis?

The elimination of the US de minimis exemption on May 2, 2025 was a body blow to Temu's original business model. Daily de minimis parcel volumes entering the US dropped 85% — from approximately 4 million to 600,000 per day [E30]. CBP director Chris Mabelitini confirmed: "We've gone from almost 4 million a day to maybe around 600,000 a day" [E30]. The US previously processed 1.36 billion de minimis parcels annually, valued at $64.6 billion, with China accounting for 60% [E30].

### The User Impact

Temu's US daily active users dropped 52-58% from approximately 70 million to 30 million in May 2025 [E29]. US monthly active users fell 51% to 40.2 million between March and June [E29]. US ad spend collapsed 95% YoY in May [E29]. Temu shut down Google Shopping ads on April 9 [E29]. The App Store ranking fell from top-3 to 132nd [E29].

Recovery came faster than expected. By Prime Day (July 8-11), activity reached 60-70% of prior peak levels [E29]. By October 2025, US MAU recovered to 133.6 million — still 28% below the prior year, but a significant bounce [E29].

### The Model Pivot

Temu cycled through three business models in rapid succession:

1. **Fully managed** (Sep 2022 launch through H1 2024): Temu handles pricing, warehousing, shipping. Merchants ship to Temu's Chinese warehouse.
2. **Semi-managed** (H2 2024 onward): Merchants handle their own logistics and ship directly to consumers.
3. **Y2 model** (launched April 27, 2025): Merchants ship after confirmed orders and handle customs clearance themselves [E31].

After tariff reduction to 54% on May 12, 2025, Temu restarted the fully managed model for items that could absorb the duty [E31]. The platform now operates all three models simultaneously, routing products based on margin tolerance.

### Infrastructure Buildout

Temu operates 13 self-owned warehouses globally: 10 in Europe, 2 in the US, and 1 in Mexico [E31]. US self-operated warehouses handle an estimated 15-20% of US volume in 2025, projected to rise to 20-25% by 2026 [E31]. Temu is using German warehouses as a central European hub, pre-positioning for the EU's de minimis removal [E31][E33].

### Global Resilience Despite US Decline

The most striking data point: Temu's global MAU hit a record 530 million in August 2025, even as US users declined [E32]. Global MAU was 416.5 million in Q2 2025, up 68% YoY. Global DAU reached 70.5 million, up 65% YoY. Cumulative global downloads exceeded 1.2 billion by October 2025 [E32]. Per HSBC, 90% of global MAU now comes from outside the US [E32].

A third of Temu merchants abandoned the US market and turned to Europe or other regions after the tariff shock [E32]. This geographic rotation is the defining feature of Temu's post-de minimis strategy.

### PDD's Revenue Deceleration

The de minimis impact shows up in PDD Holdings' consolidated numbers. Revenue growth decelerated from +131% YoY in Q1 2024 to +7-10% throughout 2025 [E16]:

| Quarter | Total Revenue (RMB B) | OMS | TS | YoY Growth |
|---------|----------------------|-----|-----|------------|
| Q1 2024 | 86.8 | 42.5 | 44.4 | +131% |
| Q2 2024 | 97.1 | 49.1 | 47.9 | — |
| Q3 2024 | 99.4 | 49.4 | 50.0 | — |
| Q4 2024 | 110.6 | 57.0 | 53.6 | — |
| Q1 2025 | 95.7 | 48.7 | 47.0 | +10% |
| Q2 2025 | 104.0 | 55.7 | 48.3 | +7% |
| Q3 2025 | 108.3 | 53.3 | 54.9 | +9% |

*Source: [E16]*

Transaction services (TS) — which includes Temu's commission revenue — grew only 7-10% YoY throughout 2025, a stark deceleration from triple-digit growth. PDD does not break out Temu financials separately, making precise attribution impossible. Q1 2025 operating profit fell 38% YoY to RMB 16.1B [E17]. Management stated the company was "prepared to make short-term sacrifices and accept declines in profitability" [E17].

### The Next Regulatory Shoe

The EU plans to remove its de minimis exemption on July 1, 2026, imposing a EUR 3 flat-rate customs duty per consignment under an interim system [E33]. Full customs duties will apply once the EU customs data hub is operational (expected 2028). The UK signaled removal of its GBP 135 customs duty relief by March 2029 [E33]. Temu is pre-positioning with its 10 European warehouses and semi-managed/bulk-shipping capabilities, but the EUR 3 per-parcel duty will hit low-value shipments disproportionately.

**Caveat:** PDD does not disclose Temu's financials separately. All revenue and profitability figures for Temu specifically are estimates or inferred from PDD's consolidated results. The unit economics of Temu's model — fully managed vs. semi-managed vs. Y2 — remain opaque.

## Q4: What Do 1.28 Million Consumer Complaints Tell Us About Platform Quality?

This is our highest-conviction finding and the data most likely to surprise readers. Heimao (黑猫投诉), the consumer complaint platform operated by Sina/Weibo, processed over 1.28 million valid e-commerce complaints in 2024, up 21.51% YoY [E34]. The top four platforms — Pinduoduo, Taobao (including Tmall), JD.com, and Douyin — accounted for 83.3% of all complaints [E34].

### Complaint Share During Double 11 2025

During the 2025 Double 11 period (October 9 - November 14), total complaints reached 183,867, up 22.72% from the previous period [E35]:

| Platform | Complaint Share | Approx. Volume |
|----------|----------------|----------------|
| Douyin Shop | 28.61% | ~53,000 |
| Taobao (incl. Tmall) | 24.50% | ~45,000 |
| Pinduoduo | 18.27% | ~33,600 |
| JD.com | 16.09% | ~29,600 |

*Source: [E35]*

Douyin leads in absolute complaint volume — not surprising given its rapid GMV growth. What matters more than complaint volume is what happened when consumers complained.

### The Response Rate Gap

This is the finding that no earnings call will tell you:

| Platform | Heimao Complaint Response Rate (Double 11 2025) |
|----------|-----------------------------------------------|
| Douyin Shop | ~99% |
| Dewu, Kuaishou, Xiaohongshu | ~99% |
| Pinduoduo | ~70% |
| JD.com | <30% |
| **Taobao (incl. Tmall)** | **<10%** |

*Sources: [E35][E36]*

Taobao and Tmall have the worst complaint response rate of any major Chinese e-commerce platform — under 10%. In December 2025, Taobao and Tmall "did not respond at all" to Heimao complaints [E36]. JD.com's response rate dropped to approximately 20% in December [E36].

Douyin and smaller platforms like Dewu, Kuaishou Shop, and Xiaohongshu Shop maintain near-perfect response rates of approximately 99% [E36]. Pinduoduo sits in the middle at approximately 70% [E35].

### Why This Matters

The response rate data connects directly to Q1's merchant cost findings. Taobao merchants spend 20-40% of revenue on platform fees and traffic, leaving minimal budget for customer service infrastructure. A merchant whose traffic promotion costs consume 70% of profits [E7] cannot afford to staff a complaint resolution team. The <10% response rate is not a customer service failure — it is a predictable consequence of the traffic tax economic model.

Meanwhile, Douyin's 99% response rate reflects a platform that is actively managing merchant-consumer disputes, partly because Douyin's integrated content-commerce model gives the platform more control over the transaction process and partly because Douyin is early enough in its e-commerce lifecycle that it prioritizes reputation.

### App Store Ratings Confirm the Divergence

iOS App Store ratings retrieved via iTunes Lookup API on March 23, 2026 [E38]:

| App | Market | Rating | Total Ratings |
|-----|--------|--------|---------------|
| Pinduoduo | China | 2.9/5 | 3,383,826 |
| Taobao | China | 4.1/5 | 1,680,978 |
| Temu | US | 4.7/5 | 2,067,322 |

*Source: [E38][E51][E53][E55]*

PDD ranked #1 on Heimao's "black list" (黑榜第一) in March 2025 [E40] and carries a 2.9/5 App Store rating — the lowest of any major platform. The consumer experience on Pinduoduo's domestic platform is measurably worse than Taobao despite PDD's lower merchant costs. The irony: PDD's low-cost model attracts price-sensitive merchants who compete on price rather than quality, producing more complaints. The same model applied internationally (Temu) somehow achieves a 4.7/5 rating, likely because Temu exercises direct quality control via the fully-managed model that domestic Pinduoduo does not.

### The Refund-Only Reversal

The refund-only (仅退款) policy, which PDD pioneered in 2021 and Taobao and JD copied by late 2023, was a consumer-friendly policy that allowed buyers to receive refunds without returning products [E41]. It became an industry-wide disaster. One merchant reported a 50% after-sales rate: out of 200 orders, 20 were refund-only and 80 were returns [E41]. Fraudulent refund services emerged on Xianyu (Alibaba's secondhand marketplace) [E41].

By April 2025, major platforms announced cancellation of refund-only. By July 2025, all platforms had terminated the policy [E41]. Regulators summoned platforms, criticizing the "race to the bottom" [E41]. PDD now supports seller-consumer negotiation as the first step, with platform intervention only if negotiation fails [E42]. Taobao is providing expedited appeal channels and upgraded AI for abnormal refund detection [E42]. JD removed language stating "platform has the right to execute refund without return" [E42].

The refund-only reversal is a leading indicator of the broader regulatory pivot from pro-consumer to pro-ecosystem policy. Beijing concluded that unlimited consumer protection at merchant expense was destroying the supply side of the marketplace.

### The Complaint-Cost Nexus

Monthly complaint volumes during promotional periods are instructive. In May 2025, cumulative complaints reached 173,800, up 21% from April, with daily complaint volume rising over 30% from mid-May onward as 618 promotion pressure built [E39]. During major shopping events like 618 and Double 11, peak daily complaints exceed 5,000 [E34]. The complaint surge during promotions is not coincidental — it reflects the operational strain that promotional pricing places on merchants who are already operating at thin margins.

The top complaint categories — customer service problems, refund disputes, and fake shipping (虚假发货) [E37] — are all symptoms of merchant resource constraints. Fake shipping, where merchants mark orders as "shipped" before actually dispatching them to meet platform logistics deadlines, is a direct consequence of platforms prioritizing speed metrics over operational reality. December 2025 saw 134,798 total complaints, down 9.43% from November's post-Double 11 peak but still elevated [E37].

## Q5: Douyin's Shelf Commerce — How Far Has It Come?

Douyin set a three-year plan for shelf e-commerce: 30% of GMV in year one (2023), 40% in year two (2024), 50% in year three (2025). It hit every target [E46]. By the 618 shopping festival in mid-2025, shelf-based e-commerce reached 50% of Douyin's GMV. Shelf GMV grew 49% YoY between August 2024 and July 2025, outpacing overall Douyin e-commerce growth of 34% [E46].

### GMV Scale

Douyin's e-commerce GMV reached approximately 3.5 trillion yuan in 2024, with a 2025 target of 4.2 trillion yuan [E47]. The first 10 months of 2025 exceeded 30% growth, and annual GMV is expected to surpass 4 trillion yuan [E47]. For context:

| Platform | 2024 GMV (est.) | Growth |
|----------|----------------|--------|
| Taotian (Taobao + Tmall) | ~8T yuan | Single-digit |
| JD.com | ~4.5T yuan | ~7% |
| Douyin | ~3.5T yuan | +30-34% |

*Source: [E44][E47]*

Douyin is now roughly 44% the size of Taobao/Tmall, growing at 30-34% while Taobao grows in single digits [E44]. At these trajectories, Douyin reaches 50% of Taobao/Tmall's scale within two years.

### The Channel Mix Shift

Douyin's commerce model has evolved from influencer-dependent to diversified:

| Channel | Share of GMV (2024) | Trend |
|---------|--------------------| ------|
| Shelf-based shopping | ~40-50% | Growing fastest (49% YoY) |
| Merchant-led livestream | ~30% | Replacing influencer streams |
| Influencer-led livestream | ~30% | Declining from majority share |

*Source: [E46][E48]*

The decline of influencer dependence is strategically critical. Top influencer streamers are susceptible to controversies and command high commission rates. Merchant-led streaming and smaller streamers are more stable and cheaper [E48]. The March 2025 merger of Ocean Engine (Douyin's advertising arm) into Douyin E-commerce combined two separate algorithm teams [E49], creating a unified commerce-advertising stack that no other Chinese platform can replicate.

### Category Positioning

Double 11 2025 revealed clear category specialization [E43]:

| Category | Tmall Share | Douyin Share | JD Share |
|----------|------------|-------------|----------|
| Apparel | 57.5% | 26% | 17% |
| Mobile phones | — | — | 56.9% |
| Computers | — | — | 62.7% |

Total Double 11 2025 GMV was RMB 1.7 trillion [E43]. Douyin's 26% apparel share is remarkable given that apparel and footwear held 60% of China's e-commerce market in 2024 [E45]. Content platforms like Douyin are especially strong in beauty, fashion, and impulse categories — the highest-margin segments of e-commerce [E45].

### The Merchant Subsidy Spend

Douyin invested 32 billion yuan in merchant cost savings in 2025 [E14][E49]:

- Q1 2025: 5.5 billion yuan (3B in Jan-Feb, 2B+ in March alone)
- By August 2025: cumulative 19 billion yuan
- Full year 2025: exceeding 32 billion yuan

Nine merchant support policies were introduced early 2025, including commission waivers for product cards, promotion fee rebates, reduced shipping insurance and security deposits, and a Small Merchant Support Fund [E49]. Douyin's 2026 strategy continues this approach — reducing merchant operating costs remains the central goal [E50].

We cannot verify the shelf commerce percentages or merchant profitability claims from primary filings. The 40% primary source rate for Q5 reflects this limitation.

## The Mosaic: What Connects These Findings

### Mosaic 1: The Traffic Tax Creates a Doom Loop

The merchant cost gap (Q1), the complaint response gap (Q4), and Douyin's shelf commerce surge (Q5) are three measurements of one phenomenon: Taobao's traffic auction model is simultaneously degrading consumer experience and pushing merchants to alternatives.

When merchants spend 10-30% of revenue on traffic just to be visible [E4], they cut spending on customer service, product quality, and fulfillment. This directly produces Taobao's under-10% Heimao complaint response rate [E36]. Merchants who are barely profitable after paying for traffic cannot afford complaint resolution. Meanwhile, Douyin's shelf commerce — which replaces paid traffic with content-driven discovery — grew 49% YoY [E46]. Merchants are voting with their feet.

Alibaba's CMR deceleration from 12% to 1% over four quarters [E1] is not cyclical. It is the quantitative signal of a structural migration away from the traffic auction model. The complaint data and shelf commerce data are downstream symptoms of the same disease.

### Mosaic 2: The Food Delivery War Is Really an Attention War

Alibaba is not spending RMB 50B on food delivery because food delivery is profitable [E21]. At AOVs of RMB 50-65 with rider costs of RMB 7-9 per order [E24], the unit economics will never work at current pricing. The real ROI is daily user engagement.

The August 7 data point proves this: a milk tea promotion drove Taobao's DAU above Pinduoduo's by 100 million [E22] — something that had not happened organically. Douyin already solved the daily engagement problem through content. Alibaba is trying to replicate Douyin's engagement-to-commerce pipeline using food delivery instead of short video. JD's food delivery entry (5-8% share [E22]) is strategically incoherent — it lacks both the content engine and the local commerce ambition to justify the investment.

### Mosaic 3: Beijing's Regulatory Pivot Is a Margin Catalyst

The refund-only reversal (Q4), SAMR's fee regulation (Q1), and the food delivery regulatory interventions (Q2) are not independent policy actions. They are a coordinated pivot from pro-consumer to pro-ecosystem policy.

Beijing concluded that the race to the bottom — unlimited subsidies, refund-without-return, below-cost traffic — was destroying merchant viability. The 50% after-sales rate merchant [E41] is the poster child. The April 2026 Platform Pricing Rules, the January 2026 Anti-Monopoly Committee statement on food delivery [E23], and the completed refund-only reversal [E41] form a coherent regulatory framework that constrains all three wars simultaneously. This is the most underappreciated margin catalyst in Chinese e-commerce for 2026.

Consider the timeline: SAMR summoned food delivery companies in May 2025 [E23]. SAMR released the 28-article fee compliance guide the same month [E15]. The Anti-Unfair Competition Law revision passed in June 2025. Platforms reversed refund-only by July 2025 [E41]. The State Council's Anti-Monopoly Committee weighed in on food delivery in January 2026 [E23]. Platform Pricing Rules take effect April 2026. This is not ad hoc enforcement — it is a sequenced regulatory campaign that began with soft guidance and is culminating in binding rules. The market has not priced in the cumulative effect because each individual action looked incremental.

The profit swing from +35.8B to -24.3B yuan [E19] was driven by a subsidy war that regulators have explicitly targeted [E23]. Meituan retains 55-58% share [E22] and scale advantages in rider network density. As subsidy intensity moderates under regulatory pressure, Meituan's fixed-cost leverage reasserts itself.

### Prediction 3: PDD's Revenue Growth Re-Accelerates to 15-20% by Mid-2026

**Conviction:** Medium (55%)

The rules ban duplicate charging, require fee transparency, and protect merchant pricing rights [E15]. Combined with anti-involution provisions, the framework constrains subsidy wars across the board. Every platform has already begun shifting rhetoric toward "merchant ecosystem health."

*What would change our mind:* If enforcement is weak; if platforms find workarounds via new fee categories.

## What We Couldn't Answer

**Precise per-order subsidy amounts in food delivery.** No platform publicly discloses how much it subsidizes each food delivery order. Meituan's per-order losses of 1.4-3 yuan [E25] are analyst estimates.

**PDD/Temu financial separation.** PDD does not break out Temu's revenue, costs, or losses. All Temu-specific profitability analysis is inferred from consolidated PDD results.

**ByteDance/Douyin financials.** ByteDance is private. All GMV, revenue, and margin estimates for Douyin come from secondary sources (36kr, kr-asia).

**Heimao data per-GMV normalization.** Douyin leads in complaint share (28.61% [E35]), but without precise GMV figures for each platform, we cannot normalize complaints per-yuan of GMV. Douyin's higher complaint share may simply reflect its faster growth.

**Chinese express carrier platform attribution.** ZTO, YTO, Yunda, and STO publish total parcel volumes but do not attribute volumes to specific e-commerce platforms [E57][E58][E59][E60]. Modeling platform-level logistics costs from carrier data requires assumptions we cannot verify.

**Live merchant surveys.** The Heimao scraper was blocked. All complaint data comes from Heimao's published annual and monthly reports, not direct scraping. Merchant cost data comes from published analyses, not direct survey.
